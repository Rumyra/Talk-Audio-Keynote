Picture of physical formats

One thing that's made me think of it more is how much I miss physical formats. Now I'm sure there's a great deal of people in this room still big into their physical formats for audio. However I gave this up a while ago. And it was only recently did I start thinking about it again.

LPs

I'm of the age where how I consume audio has change dramatically. My first experience of listening to music was my parents LPs. Routing through this huge battered cardboard boxes. Repeat playing The Beatles, ABBA, DISCO. There's something insatiable about being a child isn't there, where you can just repeat something over, and over, and over. Play a record, watch a movie, actually complete Donkey Kong.

I was talking to my mum about this and she said, what was your favourite LP - and I said DISCO and she said - we didn't have that, we were cool

Apparently definitions of cool change with time as well

Tapes

I remember begging my mother my own stereo when I was about 11. I had discovered tape casettes. If I'm honest, this is my ultimate format. There are reasons why all formats are good, but this one is my format.

Pencil, ruler, tape

I mean is there any other way of listening to audio that whne it breaks you can fix it with items in your pencil case. And yes - I have sticky tape with me. Right now!

Why cassettes

But those cases, when you opened them and took out the cassette and slid it into the carridge and closed it. All of that mechanical motion is so satisfying. Buttons - pressing buttons is great. Play, pause - up and down. Stop, eject when it clicks back out again. Getting old cassettes and putting tape over the corners so you could record whatever songs you wanted off the radio. I was 12 and I was curating mix tapes.

Minidisk

Then CDs and minidisks came along. Now I'm sure if I'd had the funds I would have got into mini disks - they were more compact. Luckily I could only afford one, and that was for when I studied Music Technology at college and the track I composed and recorded in a studio was mixed down to it. I still have it and am I glad I have nothing to play it on! No one needs to hear that.

Minidisk

Everyone was excited by laser disks

I never took to CDs. I experienced them for the most part as computer data and well, they scratch and broke and got corrupted. They took up room. I was still playing tapes.

By this time I could drive and I mean I never had a car with anything apart from a tape player. Cars with CD players were - well posh, and it wasn't long before mp3 players came out and as we well know, if you wanted to play mp3s in your car - you could do that just as well with a tape player.

MP3 player

And it was downhill from there really. My first mp3 player was something mb. It held about 8 tracks iirc.

Napster/Winamp

And I hit uni and we all hit napster. But we were still sharing music with each other. Making winamp playlists. Even those of us with shiny ipods.

What then?

And then there was a huge gap, I felt I lost my way. I had my mp3 collection and that was that. All that happened when streaming came around was I collated all the mp3s in my already collection that was sitting around random dusty hard drives.

Sharing

The thing about a physical format is you can give it to someone. Streaming open up a whole new world for us and yet no one gave me music.

I have spoke about this with a number of friends recently and one of them looked so sad and just said. Yes it used to be my favourite thing to give to a girl I fancied. I would just make them a mix CD. I mean I was like CD? Give me a tape bucko. But I got his point.

Muxtape

And people have tried this. There have been a few apps trying to emulate the old mix tape by curating a streamable playlist which you can share with a significant other. However they've never caught on. We're just too ok with everything on demand.

Phone

(PHONE) this, this is our player now. This our stereo, this is our bombox. I mean god forbid the next time I take a train journey and someone is blasting out trap on this, I am actually going to carry on my boombox and pop in a tape cassette and even with its dodgy speakers strut down the ilse and find the (person who is doing it) and do an english glare, maybe even a shake.

Phone

This phone tho. It's an interesting thing that allows us to play music. And not just to stream. It's been a creative concept for quite sometime but harking back to these past 12 months some other interesting things happened. Three people I know who also build things with code all in very quick succession made javascript based apps that played things. This isn't a new concept and is proving to be very common, especially in the realm of generative art which is increasing in popularity.
